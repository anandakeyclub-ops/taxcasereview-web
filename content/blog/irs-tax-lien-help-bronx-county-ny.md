## How Federal Tax Liens Work in New York

The IRS doesn't file a lien the moment you owe money. First, they assess the tax and send you a Notice and Demand for Payment. If you don't pay within 10 days, the lien automatically exists—but it's not yet public. The IRS then sends a Final Notice of Intent to Levy and Notice of Your Right to a Hearing (CP90 or LT11). Only after you've ignored multiple notices does the IRS file the lien publicly with Bronx County. In New York, where many residents work multiple jobs, run cash businesses in neighborhoods like Belmont and Hunts Point, or deal with complex self-employment taxes, tax debts can snowball quickly. Independent contractors, gig workers, and small business owners often face the biggest surprises when quarterly estimated taxes go unpaid. Once that lien is filed in Bronx County, it shows up on credit reports, property records, and business filings. Ignoring it only makes matters worse—the IRS will move toward bank levies and wage garnishments next.

## Your Resolution Options

**Installment Agreement**: This is a monthly payment plan that lets you pay off your tax debt over time. The IRS offers various plans depending on how much you owe. If your debt is under $50,000, you can often set up a plan online. Larger debts require financial documentation, and the IRS will want to see your income, expenses, and assets before approving terms.

**Offer in Compromise**: This program allows you to settle your tax debt for less than the full amount. The IRS accepts an offer only when they believe it's the most they can reasonably collect from you. You'll need to prove you can't pay the full amount through income or assets. Results vary. Every situation is unique. This option requires extensive paperwork and a realistic assessment of your finances.

**Penalty Abatement**: Many Bronx County taxpayers don't realize that penalties can account for 25% or more of their total debt. If you have a clean compliance history or can show reasonable cause—serious illness, death in the family, natural disaster—the IRS may remove penalties entirely. This won't remove the lien, but it reduces what you owe significantly.

**Lien Withdrawal**: Even after you pay your debt, the lien remains on public record for years unless you request withdrawal. Under the IRS Fresh Start program, you may qualify for lien withdrawal if you enter into a Direct Debit Installment Agreement and meet certain conditions. Withdrawal removes the public Notice of Federal Tax Lien, which can help restore your credit faster.

**Currently Not Collectible**: If you're facing genuine financial hardship—unemployment, medical issues, basic living expenses exceeding income—the IRS may temporarily classify your account as Currently Not Collectible. Collection activity stops, though interest and penalties continue accruing. This gives you breathing room to get back on your feet.
