The IRS follows a specific process before filing a tax lien in Martin County. First, they assess your tax liability and send you a Notice and Demand for Payment. ## Your Resolution Options

**Installment Agreement**: This allows you to pay your tax debt over time through monthly payments. The IRS offers various payment plans, including long-term agreements that can extend up to 72 months. Once you're in a compliant payment plan, the IRS may allow you to conduct business and manage your finances more normally, though the lien typically remains until the debt is paid in full.

**Offer in Compromise**: This program lets you settle your tax debt for less than you owe, but only if you meet strict criteria. The IRS examines your income, expenses, and asset equity to determine what you can realistically pay. Results vary. Every situation is unique. Approval rates are low, but for qualifying taxpayers, it's the best resolution.

**Penalty Abatement**: The IRS adds substantial penalties to unpaid taxes—sometimes 25% or more. If you have reasonable cause for late payment (medical emergency, natural disaster, or other hardship), you may qualify to have these penalties removed. This won't eliminate your tax debt, but it reduces the total amount owed.

**Lien Withdrawal**: Different from a lien release, a withdrawal removes the public Notice of Federal Tax Lien as if it was never filed. You might qualify if you've entered a Direct Debit Installment Agreement, paid your debt in full, or if the lien was filed in error. This helps repair credit damage faster than a standard release.

**Currently Not Collectible Status**: If paying your tax debt would create financial hardship, the IRS may temporarily halt collection efforts. Your account is marked as Currently Not Collectible, though interest continues to accrue. The IRS reviews your financial situation periodically and may resume collection if your circumstances improve.
