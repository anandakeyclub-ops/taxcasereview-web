## How Federal Tax Liens Work in Texas

The IRS follows a specific process before filing a lien. First, they assess your tax and send you a bill (Notice and Demand for Payment). If you don't pay within ten days, the lien automatically takes effect. The IRS then files a Notice of Federal Tax Lien with Denton County, making it public. Texas doesn't have state income tax, but federal liens still affect Texas property owners severely because real estate is often people's largest asset. In Denton County, where many residents work in healthcare, education, or own small businesses, tax debt often stems from self-employment taxes, unfiled returns, or payroll tax issues. Once filed, the lien remains until the debt is paid in full or the statute of limitations expires—usually ten years. During that time, interest compounds daily, and the IRS can proceed with more aggressive collection like wage garnishment or bank levies. Ignoring the lien doesn't make it disappear; it makes your situation worse and limits your options.

## Your Resolution Options

**Installment Agreement**: You can set up a monthly payment plan with the IRS to pay your debt over time. This prevents levies and shows good faith, though the lien typically stays in place until you pay the balance down significantly. Plans can extend up to 72 months depending on the amount owed.

**Offer in Compromise**: This program lets you settle your tax debt for less than the full amount if you qualify based on your income, expenses, and asset equity. The IRS accepts roughly 40% of applications, so proper preparation is critical. Results vary. Every situation is unique.

**Penalty Abatement**: The IRS can remove penalties if you have reasonable cause—illness, natural disaster, bad tax advice, or a clean compliance history. Removing penalties can reduce your balance by 25% or more, making the debt manageable without the lien necessarily being withdrawn.

**Lien Withdrawal**: Even after paying your balance, the lien remains on your credit. A withdrawal removes it from public record as though it never existed. You can also request withdrawal through Direct Debit Installment Agreements or if the lien impedes tax collection.

**Currently Not Collectible Status**: If paying anything would create financial hardship, the IRS can temporarily halt collection. The lien stays filed, but enforced collection stops while you're in this status, giving you breathing room to recover financially.
