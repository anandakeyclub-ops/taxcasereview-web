## What an IRS Tax Lien Means for Broward County Residents

A federal tax lien is the government's legal claim against your property when you owe back taxes. In Broward County, the IRS files these liens with the Clerk of the Circuit Court in Fort Lauderdale, making your tax debt a matter of public record. This means anyone checking public records—from mortgage lenders to potential employers—can see you owe the IRS. The lien attaches to everything you own: your home in Coral Springs, your car, your boat docked in Hollywood, even business assets. It destroys your credit score, typically dropping it 100 points or more overnight. If you own property in Broward County and want to sell or refinance, the IRS must be paid first from the proceeds. This isn't just paperwork—it's a serious legal claim that follows you until the debt is resolved.

## How Federal Tax Liens Work in Florida

The IRS doesn't file a lien immediately after you owe taxes. First, you'll receive a Notice and Demand for Payment. If you don't pay or respond within ten days, the IRS assesses the tax and sends a Final Notice of Intent to Levy. Once that 30-day window closes, they file the Notice of Federal Tax Lien with the Broward County Clerk's office in Fort Lauderdale. At this point, it becomes public record and impacts your credit report. In Florida, many taxpayers I worked with as a revenue officer were business owners in industries like hospitality, real estate, and marine services—fields with seasonal income fluctuations that can create tax payment gaps. Florida also has no state income tax, which sometimes leads residents to underestimate their federal obligations. Once filed, the lien remains on your property and credit for the entire time you owe, plus seven years after you pay in full. Ignoring it only makes things worse, as the IRS can move to levy your bank accounts or garnish wages.

## Your Resolution Options

**Installment Agreement**: This is a monthly payment plan where you pay your tax debt over time, typically 72 months or less. The IRS will continue to charge interest and penalties until you pay in full, but an installment agreement prevents levies and shows you're making good faith efforts to resolve the debt. Many Broward County taxpayers qualify for streamlined agreements without extensive financial disclosure.

**Offer in Compromise**: This program allows you to settle your tax debt for less than you owe if you can demonstrate paying the full amount would create financial hardship. The IRS evaluates your income, expenses, and asset equity. While this sounds appealing, the IRS rejects most offers that aren't properly prepared and documented. Results vary. Every situation is unique.

**Penalty Abatement**: The IRS can remove penalties (but not the underlying tax or interest) if you have reasonable cause—serious illness, bad tax advice, natural disaster, or a clean compliance history. First-time penalty abatement is available if you haven't had penalties in the previous three years. This can reduce your total debt by 25% or more in some cases.

**Lien Withdrawal**: Even after paying your tax debt, the lien remains on public record for seven years unless you request a withdrawal. You can also request withdrawal if you enter certain payment agreements or if the lien was filed in error. A withdrawal removes the public notice and helps repair credit damage faster than simply waiting for the lien to expire.

**Currently Not Collectible Status**: If you're facing genuine financial hardship—unemployment, medical issues, or expenses exceeding income—the IRS may temporarily halt collection efforts. Your account is marked as uncollectible, stopping levies and wage garnishments. The debt doesn't disappear, but it gives you breathing room to get back on your feet.
