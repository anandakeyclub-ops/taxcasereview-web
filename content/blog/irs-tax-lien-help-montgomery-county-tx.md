# IRS Tax Lien Help in Montgomery County, Texas: What to Do Right Now

*Former IRS officer explains exactly what to do when the IRS files a tax lien against your Montgomery County property—and how to get it resolved before a levy hits your bank account.*

## What an IRS Tax Lien Means for Montgomery County Residents

A federal tax lien is the IRS's legal claim against your property when you owe back taxes. Once filed with the Montgomery County Clerk's office in Conroe, it becomes public record—visible to anyone who searches property records. This lien attaches to everything you own: your home, vehicles, bank accounts, and even future property you acquire. In Montgomery County's competitive real estate market, a tax lien can destroy your credit score, prevent you from refinancing your home, and make it nearly impossible to sell property without paying the IRS first. The lien stays in place until you pay your full tax debt or make acceptable arrangements with the IRS. If you've received a Notice of Federal Tax Lien, you're already deep into the IRS collection process—but you still have options.

## How Federal Tax Liens Work in Texas

The IRS doesn't file a lien overnight. You've already received multiple notices: a balance due notice, then a Final Notice of Intent to Levy. If you ignored those warnings, the IRS files the lien with Montgomery County to establish priority over other creditors. In Texas, where many residents work in oil and gas, construction, or own small businesses, tax debts often pile up during industry downturns or cash flow problems. Once the lien is filed, the IRS sends you a Notice of Federal Tax Lien Filing and Your Right to a Hearing. You have just 30 days from that notice to request a Collection Due Process hearing—a critical window to dispute the lien or propose alternatives. Miss that deadline, and your options narrow significantly. The lien also appears on your credit report, typically dropping your score by 100 points or more, which affects everything from car loans to insurance rates across Montgomery County.

## Your Resolution Options

**Installment Agreement**: This is a monthly payment plan that lets you pay your tax debt over time. The IRS offers streamlined agreements for debts under $50,000, often approved quickly without extensive financial documentation. Once you're in a valid installment agreement, the IRS won't levy your wages or bank accounts, though the lien typically remains until you pay the balance in full.

**Offer in Compromise**: This program lets you settle your tax debt for less than you owe if you can prove you can't pay the full amount. The IRS examines your income, expenses, and assets to determine what you can reasonably pay. It's not easy to qualify—only about 40% of offers are accepted—but for taxpayers facing genuine financial hardship in Montgomery County, it can eliminate decades of tax debt.

**Penalty Abatement**: Interest keeps running on tax debt, but penalties often make up 25% or more of what you owe. If you have reasonable cause—serious illness, natural disaster, bad tax advice—you can request First-Time Penalty Abatement or reasonable cause relief. Removing penalties can save thousands of dollars and make your remaining debt manageable.

**Lien Withdrawal**: Different from lien release, a withdrawal removes the public Notice of Federal Tax Lien as if it was never filed. You might qualify if you've paid your debt, entered a Direct Debit Installment Agreement, or the lien was filed in error. A withdrawal helps repair your credit faster than waiting for automatic release.

**Currently Not Collectible Status**: If paying anything toward your tax debt would create genuine financial hardship, the IRS can temporarily halt collection efforts. You'll need to prove your monthly income barely covers necessary living expenses. While in CNC status, the IRS won't levy your assets, though interest continues accruing and the lien remains in place.
