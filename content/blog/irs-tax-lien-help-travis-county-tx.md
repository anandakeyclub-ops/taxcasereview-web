# IRS Tax Lien Help in Travis County, Texas: What to Do Right Now

*Former IRS officer explains exactly what Travis County taxpayers should do when the IRS files a federal tax lien and how to get it removed from public records.*

## What an IRS Tax Lien Means for Travis County Residents

A federal tax lien is the government's legal claim against your property when you neglect or fail to pay a tax debt. Once the IRS files a Notice of Federal Tax Lien with the Travis County Clerk's office in Austin, it becomes public record. This means anyone—mortgage lenders, potential employers, landlords—can see you owe back taxes. The lien attaches to everything you own in Travis County: your home, car, bank accounts, and even future property you acquire. Your credit score typically drops 100 points or more the moment the lien hits your report. If you're planning to sell your Austin home or refinance, the lien must be addressed first. The IRS gets paid before you see any proceeds from the sale.

## How Federal Tax Liens Work in Texas

The IRS doesn't file a tax lien immediately. First, they assess the tax you owe and send you a Notice and Demand for Payment. If you ignore that notice for several weeks, they'll send a Final Notice of Intent to Levy and Notice of Your Right to a Hearing. This gives you 30 days to respond. If you still don't act, the IRS files the lien with Travis County. Texas has a robust economy with many self-employed professionals, tech workers, and small business owners in Austin who sometimes fall behind on quarterly estimated payments. The IRS knows Texas has no state income tax, so they're particularly aggressive about collecting federal taxes here. Once filed, the lien remains public record until the debt is fully paid and released, which can take years. The longer it sits, the more damage it does to your financial life.

## Your Resolution Options

You have several ways to resolve an IRS tax lien in Travis County, and the right choice depends on your financial situation.

**Installment Agreement** — This is a monthly payment plan that lets you pay your tax debt over time, typically 72 months or less. Once you're in good standing with payments for several months, you may qualify for lien withdrawal. The IRS removes the public lien filing even though you still owe the debt, which helps restore your credit faster.

**Offer in Compromise** — This program lets you settle your tax debt for less than the full amount owed. The IRS accepts an offer when they determine it's the most they can reasonably collect from you. Approval rates are low—around 30%—but if you qualify and your offer is accepted, the lien is released within 30 days of your final payment.

**Penalty Abatement** — If penalties make up a significant portion of your tax debt, you might qualify to have them removed based on reasonable cause or first-time penalty abatement. Reducing your total balance can make payment plans more manageable and may prevent future collection action.

**Lien Withdrawal** — This removes the public Notice of Federal Tax Lien, which helps your credit score recover. You might qualify if you enter a Direct Debit Installment Agreement, if the lien was filed in error, or if withdrawal helps the IRS collect the debt faster.

**Currently Not Collectible Status** — If you can prove that paying anything would create financial hardship, the IRS may temporarily halt collection efforts. Your account is marked as uncollectible, and while the lien remains filed, the IRS won't actively pursue levies or garnishments during this period.

I saw these mistakes repeatedly during my years as an IRS revenue officer. The biggest one is waiting too long to act. Many Austin taxpayers receive the Final Notice and think they have more time than they do. By the time they call for help, the lien is already filed and public. This guarantees the worst outcome—the lien gets filed, and levy action follows soon after. The IRS interprets silence as refusal to cooperate, which makes them less willing to work with you later.
