## What an IRS Tax Lien Means for Bexar County Residents

A federal tax lien is the IRS's legal claim against everything you own when you fail to pay a tax debt. In Bexar County, the IRS files these liens with the County Clerk's office in San Antonio, making your tax problems part of the public record. This lien attaches to your current assets and any property you acquire while the debt remains unpaid—your home, vehicles, business equipment, and even future inheritances. Your credit score typically drops 100 points or more the moment the lien becomes public. If you're planning to refinance your San Antonio home, sell property, or apply for business financing anywhere in Bexar County, that lien will create serious obstacles. Title companies won't clear transactions until the IRS lien is addressed, and lenders view it as one of the most serious red flags on your financial profile.

## How Federal Tax Liens Work in Texas

The IRS follows a specific process before filing a tax lien in Bexar County. First, they assess your tax and send you a bill—the Notice and Demand for Payment. If you don't pay the full amount within 10 days, the lien automatically attaches to your property, though it's not yet public. The IRS then sends a Notice of Federal Tax Lien, which they file with the Bexar County Clerk. From that point, your debt appears in public records searchable by creditors, employers, and anyone else. Texas doesn't have state income tax, but that doesn't protect you from federal liens. In fact, many Bexar County taxpayers face liens due to unfiled returns from years of 1099 contractor work—common in San Antonio's growing healthcare, military contracting, and hospitality sectors. Once filed, the lien remains in place until the debt is paid, the collection period expires, or you negotiate a specific resolution. Ignoring the lien leads to bank levies, wage garnishments, and seizure of assets.

## Your Resolution Options

**Installment Agreement**: This is the most common resolution for an IRS tax lien in Bexar County. You agree to pay your debt in monthly installments over time, typically up to 72 months. The lien remains in place, but you stop the IRS from levying your bank accounts or wages. Once you've made three consecutive payments and meet other criteria, you may qualify for lien withdrawal.

**Offer in Compromise**: This program allows you to settle your tax debt for less than the full amount owed, but the IRS only accepts about 40% of applications. You must prove you cannot pay the full debt before the collection statute expires, and the IRS examines your income, expenses, and asset equity in detail. If accepted, the lien is released within 30 days of your final payment.

**Penalty Abatement**: The IRS adds failure-to-pay and failure-to-file penalties that can increase your debt by 25% or more. First-time penalty abatement or reasonable cause abatement can eliminate these penalties. While this doesn't remove the lien immediately, reducing your total debt makes other resolution options more achievable and affordable.

**Lien Withdrawal**: Different from lien release, withdrawal removes the public Notice of Federal Tax Lien as if it was never filed. You may qualify if you're in a Direct Debit Installment Agreement, the lien was filed prematurely or not according to IRS procedures, or withdrawal helps you pay your debt faster.

**Currently Not Collectible**: If you're facing genuine financial hardship—income barely covers necessary living expenses—the IRS may temporarily stop collection efforts. Your account is marked Currently Not Collectible, though interest and penalties continue accruing and the lien remains filed. This buys you time while you get back on your feet.
